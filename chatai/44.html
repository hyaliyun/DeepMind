answer:In a screenplay, using italics can serve various purposes, but it's essential not to overuse them as they can distract the reader. Italics could be used to highlight a word or a phrase within dialogue to emphasize a particular intonation, underlying subtext, or emotional beat that the writer wants to convey more strongly. However, for entire dialogues or interactions, it's less common to use italics. Usually, the tone, volume, or emotional intensity of a character's speech is described in the parentheticals (direction for the actor that appears directly below the character's name) or in the action lines (the descriptive passages of the screenplay). For example: ``` ED (loudly, with passion) Imagine, if you will, a world free from the rigors of time! ``` The above emphasizes that Ed is speaking with particular enthusiasm and volume. If you want to convey a technical term, foreign phrase, inner thought, or something that needs to stand out in the delivery, you could then resort to italics: ``` MARIA Remember, it's pronounced *phoenix*. ``` In the case of internal monologue or thoughts, italics can be useful to distinguish these from spoken dialogue, especially if the screenplay style allows for such internal reflections to be presented as voice-overs. ``` ED (thinking, *voice-over*) Is this what crossing the Rubicon feels like? ``` It is worth mentioning that screenplay formatting standards do not typically involve italicizing large portions of dialogue. Instead, clear and concise descriptions and parentheticals should be used to guide the reader and actor on the delivery of lines. The emotional weight, rhythm, and pacing of dialogue should be evident in the context and the way the lines are written, not primarily through formatting tricks like italics. Ultimately, the use of italics should be consistent and follow the specific style guidelines of the production company or the screenplay competition to which you are submitting. Always make sure your use of italics is purposeful and enhances the readability of the script rather than detracting from it.
